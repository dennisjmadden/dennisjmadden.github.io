Athletic monkey mind

By Dennis Madden

We all want to be good at something.

Middle distance track. Strength training. Olympic lifting. Track cycling. SCUBA diving. Rock climbing. Brazilian jiu-jitsu. Movnat. Track cycling. Triathlon. Power lifting. Crossfit. Mountaineering. Snowboarding. Split boarding. Free diving. Alpine skiing. XC mtn biking. Downhill and enduro. Trials riding. BMX. Open water swimming. Marathons. Ultra marathons. Gymnastics. Biathlon. Fencing. Boxing. Wang chung. Judo. Trail running. Kendo. Sailing. Running. Backpacking. Yoga (2 hot yoga sessions so far!).

This is a quick brain dump of things since I thought I should "get into" since I started college a little more than 10 years ago.

Things I have spent $$$ on. Things I did but don't realistically do today. (As in multiple times per month, or high priority during weekends). See the relationship between this line and the one above here? Things I actually do.

There are many things I identity with and have invested time and money in, but upon reflection, I haven't done these in quite some time.

Now I see why I'm not as good as I fantasize I can be at any one thing. This also puts into perspective what I actually do vs. what I think I do. I've been having this conversation with my brother about several different topics lately (not just sports or hobbies). If I really want to be fencing right now, I'd be out there doing it. But I'm not.

Sometime l whine to myself that I "deserve" to be a better runner, but look at all the other stuff I've clogged my head with over the past 12 years. I am a gear hoarder. Maybe keeping all this equipment on hand keeps a little fuel on the delusion wild fire in my head. Many of this stuff I could probably rent. Take diving for example. I bought all this stuff but I will likely only dive on vacations, a situation where I will rent anyway! Same goes for a lot of the other stuff on this list. A friend of mine rented a road bike for an Ironman we did...and he kicked ass. Granted he has bikes at home to train on, but the point is he did fine without the fanciest of gear.

Monkey mind is a Buddhist term describing an unsettled state. Does this describe you? Want to be good (and probably save yourself some $$)? Audit your monkey mind, find out what you actually do vs. what you think you'd like to do. Maybe clarity will help you up your game. Of course this has to be balanced with exploring new activities.

When you open the door be mindful not to let all the monkeys in.
